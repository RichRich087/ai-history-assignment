  • 1956

    The Birth of AI

    The Dartmouth Conference is widely considered as the birth of AI. John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon proposed that "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."

  • 1966

    ELIZA

    Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT developed ELIZA, an early natural language processing computer program that could simulate conversation by pattern matching and substitution methodology. ELIZA's most famous script, DOCTOR, simulated a psychotherapist.

  • 1968-1970

    SHRDLU

    SHRDLU, developed by Terry Winograd at MIT, was a natural language understanding computer program. It could interpret commands given in natural language to a virtual robot in a small world of blocks.

  • 1972

    MYCIN

    MYCIN, developed at Stanford University, was an early expert system that used artificial intelligence to identify bacteria causing severe infections and to recommend antibiotics, their dose, and schedule.

  • 1974-1980

    The AI Winter

    A period of disappointment in AI research after initial enthusiasm and funding. The promises of AI were not met as quickly as expected, leading to reduced funding and interest in AI.

  • 1986

    The Revival of Neural Networks

    The publication of the book "Parallel Distributed Processing" by David Rumelhart, James McClelland, and the PDP Research Group sparked a resurgence in interest in neural networks and connectionism.

  • 1997

    IBM's Deep Blue

    IBM's Deep Blue became the first computer chess-playing system to win a chess game against a reigning world chess champion, Garry Kasparov.

  • 2004-2005

    The DARPA Grand Challenge

    The U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) held a driverless car competition, which significantly advanced research in autonomous vehicles.

  • 2011

    IBM's Watson Wins Jeopardy

    IBM's Watson, a question-answering system, famously won a game of Jeopardy against two of the show's greatest champions, demonstrating significant advancements in natural language processing.

  • 2016

    AlphaGo Defeats Go Champion

    Google's DeepMind developed AlphaGo, a computer program that defeated world champion Go player Lee Sedol. This was a significant event as Go is a complex game with more possible moves than there are atoms in the universe, requiring more than just brute force computation to win.
